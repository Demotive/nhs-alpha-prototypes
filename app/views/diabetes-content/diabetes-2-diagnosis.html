Understanding your diabetes type 2 diagnosis

You'll need to have some tests to find out whether you have type 2 diabetes. If diagnosed, there are things you can do to help with any shock and confusion you might feel.

Test you’ll need to get a diagnosis

To find out if you have diabetes you usually have to go through the following steps:

  1. Speak to your GP about your symptoms.
  2. If your GP thinks you might have diabetes they may check your urine and ask for a blood test to check your blood sugar levels. It usually takes about X weeks for the blood test results to come back.
  3. If the tests show that you have diabetes, your GP will ask you to come in again to explain what will happen next. You’ll be most likely be put on medication straight away.

Write down any questions you want to ask your GP. Some things you might want to ask could be:

  • why could my symptoms mean that I have diabetes?
  • why do I have to do a urine and blood test?
  • who will do the tests?
  • is there anything I should do or avoid in the the meantime, eg certain foods?

If you’re diagnosed with diabetes

If your blood test shows that you have diabetes, your GP will ask you to come in for another appointment.

What they’ll talk to you about in the appointment depends very much on the diagnosis and the treatment they’ll recommend.

Generally, they’ll explain the following to you:

  • what diabetes is
  • what high blood sugar means for your health
  • what medication you’ll have to take
  • your diet and exercise

Your GP might not have much time to explain the diagnosis to you. The appointment might only be 10 to 15 minutes long.

Dealing with confusion and shock after the diagnosis

It’s usually difficult to take everything in the GP tells you during the appointment. You might even be in shock.

Talk to other people

You should take some time after the diagnosis to talk to family and friends about what the GP told you.

You might want to talk to other people who have diabetes. You can do this through online forums or in support groups.

Hearing how other people manage their diabetes and what changes they had to make to their lifestyle could help you organise how you live with diabetes.

Write down your questions

During this time write down all the questions you have.

Make another appointment with your GP and take your list of questions with you.

There’s also a lot of information on diabetes available where you can read up on the condition. Some hospitals offer diabetes courses where you can learn more about the condition.

What happens after the diagnosis

Usually the following things happen after your diagnosis:

  1. Your GP will prescribe medication. It might take some time for you to get used to the medication and to find the right amounts for you.
  2. You might need to make changes to your diet and start being active.
  3. You’ll have to watch your body for certain signs to avoid other health problems.
  4. You’ll have to go for regular diabetes type 2 check ups.
